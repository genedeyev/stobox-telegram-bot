# Stobox Community Q&A — Canonical Answers

Approved answers to real questions from the Stobox community. These are the
official, Gene-approved wordings — use their facts exactly.

## 1. If Stobox moves to Base, does that mean the Arbitrum grants will end and the DefiLlama listing will soon disappear?


**Answer:**

No on both counts.

Our DefiLlama listing continues — we will keep reporting and maintaining it as before. The
listing tracks Stobox's real-world-asset activity, and that doesn't change because of where new
issuance happens.

On the network side, "moving to Base" means Base becomes the primary network for new issuance
through Stobox Compass — it does not mean we are leaving Arbitrum. Arbitrum remains a supported
network in our infrastructure, existing deployments there continue to operate, and any grant
commitments run according to their own terms. Stobox is built as multichain infrastructure:
adding a primary network is an expansion of where we operate, not an exit from where we already
are.

## 2. How many companies have been tokenized over the last six months?


**Answer:**

We don't disclose rolling client or deal counts. Two reasons: most of our engagements are with
private companies that have their own confidentiality expectations, and interim numbers taken
out of context (deals signed vs. structured vs. issued vs. live) tend to mislead more than they
inform.

What we do share publicly: named case studies and announcements when a client chooses to go
public, and aggregate milestones when we publish them officially. If a specific tokenization is
announced, you'll see it on stobox.io and our official channels — that's the reliable record.

## 3. What is the rationale behind changing the blockchain network every year?


**Answer:**

Fair question, but the premise isn't quite right — so let me address both the premise and the
substance.

Stobox has not "changed networks every year." Stobox is tokenization infrastructure, and it has
been multichain by design from early on. Over seven years we added network support in the order
the market matured: Ethereum when it was the only credible option, then lower-cost networks when
clients needed affordable issuance, then L2s as that's where activity moved. Those weren't
reversals — each network we supported continued to operate. That's what infrastructure is
supposed to do: meet issuers and investors where they are.

What's happening now with Base is different in kind, and it's actually the opposite of
chain-hopping: it's consolidation. Supporting utility-token liquidity fragmented across multiple
chains serves nobody — not holders, not integrators. So we're designating a primary home and
consolidating there, once, with a clear 1:1 migration path.

Why Base specifically? The criteria are the ones an institutional tokenization platform has to
care about: where regulated and institutional on-chain activity is concentrating, quality and
longevity of the ecosystem's backing, transaction costs that make real-world usage viable, and
the compliance tooling available on the network. Base is the strongest answer to those criteria
today, which is why new issuance through Stobox Compass is happening there primarily — while
other networks we support remain operational.

Our commitment isn't to any single chain — it's to our clients and token holders having their
assets on infrastructure that stays relevant. Network strategy follows that commitment, not the
other way around.

## 4. Looking at the Dune dashboard, there doesn't seem to have been much activity involving STBX. Does that mean the seed round has effectively failed?


**Answer:**

No — for a simple reason: there is no seed round running, so there is nothing that could have
"failed." Investment rounds are planned, and when one opens it will be announced through
official Stobox channels with its terms. Until then, no on-chain dashboard can show you the
status of a raise that hasn't started.

It's also worth understanding what a Dune dashboard can and cannot tell you about a token like
STBX. STBX is a tokenized security — Class C shares of Stobox, issued by Stobox Tokenized
Equities Ltd. It is a compliance-gated instrument held by verified investors, not a
freely-traded utility token. Low on-chain transfer velocity is the expected profile for a
security token held by long-term shareholders; raw transfer counts are the wrong metric for it.
Judging a security token by DEX-style activity metrics is like judging private-company equity by
how often the share certificates change hands.

When we have investment-round news, you will hear it from us directly — not have to infer it
from a dashboard.
